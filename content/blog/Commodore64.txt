The Commodore 64, also known as the C64, is an 8-bit home computer that was popular in the 1980s and early 1990s. It was released by Commodore International in 1982 and became one of the best-selling computers of all time, with an estimated 17 million units sold worldwide. The Commodore 64 was a significant improvement over its predecessor, the Commodore VIC-20, with improved hardware specifications and a unique feature set that made it a popular choice for both home users and game developers.

Hardware Specifications:

The Commodore 64 was based on the MOS Technology VIC-II graphics processor and the 6510 microprocessor, which was a modified version of the MOS Technology 6502 processor used in the Apple II and the Atari 400/800. The computer had 64 kilobytes of RAM, which was a significant improvement over the 3.5 kilobytes of RAM found in the VIC-20. The Commodore 64 also had 16 kilobytes of ROM, which contained the computer's basic input/output system (BIOS) and the KERNAL, the Commodore 64's operating system.

One of the unique features of the Commodore 64 was its SID (Sound Interface Device) chip, which was a revolutionary sound synthesizer that could produce a wide range of audio effects, from simple beeps and boops to complex musical compositions. The SID chip was capable of producing three channels of audio, each with its own volume control and filter options. This made the Commodore 64 one of the first home computers to feature high-quality audio capabilities, and it was a major factor in the computer's popularity among musicians and game developers.

Legacy:

The Commodore 64 was a significant factor in the growth of the home computer market in the 1980s, and it paved the way for later home computers like the Nintendo Entertainment System and the IBM PC.
